The Origins of Impressionism

Impressionism emerged in France during the late nineteenth century. Artists rejected strict realism and instead focused on light, movement, color, and ordinary life.

Claude Monet

Claude Monet was a French painter and one of the founding figures of Impressionism, a movement focused on capturing light and atmosphere rather than precise detail. He often painted the same scene multiple times under different lighting conditions to study how color and perception change. His most famous works include the Water Lilies series and Impression, Sunrise, which actually gave Impressionism its name. Monet spent much of his later life painting his garden at Giverny, turning it into a living laboratory for light and color.

Vincent van Gogh

Vincent van Gogh was a Dutch Post-Impressionist painter known for emotional intensity and bold, expressive brushwork. His art is deeply personal, often reflecting his mental struggles and emotional state. Though he sold few paintings during his lifetime, he created over 2,000 works, including iconic pieces like The Starry Night, Sunflowers, and Wheatfield with Crows. Van Gogh’s use of color and movement had a major influence on modern art, especially Expressionism.

Edgar Degas

Edgar Degas focused on movement, dancers, and scenes from modern Parisian life using unusual compositions and perspectives.

Edgar Degas was a French artist associated with Impressionism, though he preferred to be called a realist. He is best known for his paintings, drawings, and sculptures of dancers, especially ballet rehearsals and backstage moments. Degas was fascinated by movement and composition, often using unusual angles and cropped framing that felt modern and dynamic. He also painted horse races, portraits, and everyday urban life, showing a wide range of subjects beyond dance.

Pierre-Auguste Renoir

Pierre-Auguste Renoir was a French Impressionist painter known for celebrating beauty, joy, and human connection. His works often depict social gatherings, outdoor scenes, and people enjoying leisure activities. Paintings like Dance at Le Moulin de la Galette and Luncheon of the Boating Party show his interest in light, color, and atmosphere. Renoir’s style is soft and warm, with a strong focus on skin tones and the pleasure of everyday life.

Mary Cassatt

Mary Cassatt was an American painter who became a key member of the French Impressionist movement. She is best known for her intimate portrayals of mothers and children, focusing on quiet domestic moments such as bathing, reading, and caring. Her work often used soft colors and strong composition influenced by Japanese prints. Cassatt played an important role in introducing Impressionism to American collectors and helped expand opportunities for women in the art world.
